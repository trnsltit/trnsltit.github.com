“Translate It” facilitates the communication of simple questions and phrases in multiple languages, with an emphasis on user-defined content. In this, it supports those providing humanitarian aid, medical services and governmental assistance, as well as newcomers themselves.

About

Whether providing humanitarian aid, medical services or governmental assistance, many face the challenge of communicating simple messages or asking simple questions of newcomers to Germany in languages they understand. Adding to the difficulty is that a significant proportion of refugees arriving in Europe have difficulty reading and writing, even in their own languages1. For this reason, it is not always possible to depend upon written translations in order to facilitate communication. But relying on the presence of translators for 8-10+ languages at a time is rarely feasible either.

Among existing translation apps, there are three principal models. The first, like Google Translate, provides automated machine translation. While these apps can be helpful, translation accuracy for target languages such as Farsi, Kurdish and Arabic is notoriously poor.

The second model, which has been adopted by a variety of translation apps, centers on working with fixed sets of pre-written and/or pre-recorded questions. Here, a user chooses the question s/he would like to pose in the source language and then selects from a pre-determined list of target languages. Having done this, s/he can view a written translation of the question and/or play a recording of that translation. With this approach, the content translation is not produced on-the-fly, but rather pre-recorded by a human translator and saved as an unchangeable core component of the app itself.

This second approach ensures higher translation quality, but unfortunately, it also prevents users from adding any new content or adapting or reorganizing existing content. It is impossible for a user to amend or customize question lists, or to add languages or dialects that have not already been built into the app’s structure by the developers from the start. As a consequence, the app’s usefulness in new or unforeseen contexts is severely limited. Content cannot be adapted to local needs or specific user requirements. This lack of flexibility affects both usability and the practical application of the app.

A third model, which combines provided content with the possibility of creating user-defined content, is the direction we have pursued with Translate It. There are a very limited number apps working with this approach. None of those we have been able to find are web-based or compatible with multiple mobile platforms. Further, existing solutions do not fully support the customization of content structures or the addition of new translations to existing content.

1: Mason, Ben and Dennis Buchmann. 2016. ICT4Refugees. Bonn: GIZ. Available at http://www.betterplace-lab.org/wp-content/uploads/ICT4Refugees-Report.pdf

Imagine the following challenges…

  • A recently arrived refugee needs to make an appointment. The receptionist says in German, “Bitte nehmen Sie Platz. Frau Schmidt kommt bald an.” How will the refugee understand?
  • At a local “Erstaufnahmestelle” (refugee camp), there was a disease outbreak. Doctors needed to ask the same questions to a lot of people. Many of these people could read and so didn’t understand written information that was provided.

What would help both sides? Our app hopes to make a difference.

What makes our app different?

Works Offline

You can fetch pre-made recordings online, but the application continues to work even when there is no connection available.

Customizable with new Recordings

You have the ability to add a new recording anytime, anywhere, and to organize the recordings as you (not the developers) need them to be.

Compatible

It runs on multiple different devices, directly in a browser.

Organize

Organize your recordings in categories and folders to find them fast.

Because the app makes audio communication central, it simplifies ease-of-use and emphasizes human connections. We can actually look at each other when we talk, rather than having our technological devices stand in the middle.

Lastly, the app provides an immediate benefit, even if only a small number of people use the app. A large user base is not required.
This combination of features distinguishes our app from other translation apps. From its foundation, the Translate It app is structured to be adaptable to many different situations.
